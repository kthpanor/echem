TDDFT¶

Time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) shares many aspects with time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) [DHG05], and the discussion here will thus focus more on what need to be additionally considered for TDDFT. We thus recommend going over the TDHF material presented in the previous section, before studying the material presented here.

Note

  • Discuss theory here, and regularly refer to numerical examples in exercises (can skip to solution…)

  • Use this paper

  • No need to repeat numerical examples from TDHF - focus on what is new

Theory¶

Runge-Gross theorem¶

Work of Runge and Gross in 1984, where they presented the necessary theorem

For every single-particle potential \(V(r,t)\), which can be expanded in a Taylor series around \(t_0\), there exists a mapping \(G:V(r,t) \rightarrow \rho (r,t)\), invertible up to an additive time-dependent function of the potential. This mapping is defined by solving the time-dependent SE with a fixed initial state \(\phi\) and calculating the corresponding densities \(\rho (r,t)\).

Also presented theorems for pracical schemes, most important being

The exact time-dependent density can be expressed as a sum over states: \(\rho (r,t) = \sum | \phi (r,t)|^2\)

Calculate time-dependent orbitals from

\[ i \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \phi_i (r,t) = \left( -\frac{1}{2} \nabla^2 + V_{\textrm{eff}} (r,t) \right) \phi_i (r,t) \]

Requires knowledge of exact time-dependent xc-functional. Even less known than the ground-state correspondance.

Exists a one-to-one mapping between the time-dependent (external) potential and the density of the system.

Adiabatic approximation¶

Approximate the xc-functional as one which do not change over time, such that standard ones can be used. Reasonable approximations for systems changing slowly in time.

Time-dependence of enerrgy only through time-dependet density.

Search for non-adiabatic functional may be somewhat futile, as technically requires the solution of the time-dependent SE, as has been discussed.

Casida equation¶

Note

Changes when compared to TDHF, and note aspects of incorrect long-range behaviour.

  • The Hessian matrix is Hermitian and has an internal structure in terms of the matrix blocks \(\mathbf{A}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\)

  • The diagonal elements correspond to differences in energy between the single-excited determinants \(|0_i^a\rangle\) and the reference state \(|0\rangle\)

  • The \(\mathbf{A}\)-block corresponds to the CIS approximation

In the Hartree–Fock approximation, the Hessian can be expressed as the differences in orbital energies corrected by an anti-symmetrized two-electron integral

\[\begin{split} \mathbf{E}^{[2]} = \begin{pmatrix} \rule[-10pt]{0pt}{30pt} F_{st} \delta_{ij} \!-\! F_{ji}\delta_{ab} \!-\! \langle aj || bi \rangle & -\langle ab||ij \rangle \\ \rule[-10pt]{0pt}{30pt} -\langle ij||ab \rangle & F_{ts} \delta_{ij}\! -\! F_{ij} \delta_{ab} \! - \! \langle ai || bj \rangle \\ \end{pmatrix} \end{split}\]

  • The anti-symmetrized ERI gives the correct asymptotic behavior for charge-transfer excitations

  • At the level of DFT, the exchange–correlation functional is introduced and the asymptotic behavior becomes incorrect

The electronic Hessian takes the form:

(160)¶\[\begin{eqnarray} \mathbf{E}^{[2]} & = & \begin{pmatrix} \rule[-5pt]{0pt}{20pt} \rule{5pt}{0pt} \mathbf{A} & \mathbf{B} \rule{5pt}{0pt} \\ \rule[-10pt]{0pt}{25pt} \rule{5pt}{0pt} \mathbf{B}^* & \mathbf{A}^* \\ \end{pmatrix} \end{eqnarray}\]

In TDDFT, with a general hybrid functional

\[\begin{split} A_{ia,jb} = \delta_{ij} \delta_{ab} (\epsilon_a - \epsilon_i) + (ia|jb) + c_{\textrm{HF}}(ij|ab) + (1-c_{\textrm{HF}})(ia|f_{\textrm{xc}}|jb)\\ B_{ia,jb} = (ia|bj) + c_{\textrm{HF}}(ib|aj) + (1-c_{\textrm{HF}})(ia|f_{\textrm{xc}}|bj) \end{split}\]

In the limits we retrieve the TDHF blocks (\(c_{\textrm{HF}} = 1\)) and pure TDDFT (\(c_{\textrm{HF}} = 0\)).

Compare what we have derived to the version used in the Casida equation.

Non-Hermitian pseudo-eigenvalue equation, with excitation energies and transition densities as eigenvalues and eigenvectors:

(161)¶\[\begin{eqnarray} \begin{pmatrix} \rule[-5pt]{0pt}{20pt} \rule{5pt}{0pt} \mathbf{A} & \mathbf{B} \rule{5pt}{0pt} \\ \rule[-10pt]{0pt}{25pt} \rule{5pt}{0pt} \mathbf{B}^* & \mathbf{A}^* \\ \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \rule[-5pt]{0pt}{20pt} \rule{5pt}{0pt} \mathbf{X} \rule{5pt}{0pt} \\ \rule[-10pt]{0pt}{25pt} \rule{5pt}{0pt} \mathbf{Y} \\ \end{pmatrix} = \omega \begin{pmatrix} \rule[-5pt]{0pt}{20pt} \rule{5pt}{0pt} \mathbf{1} & \mathbf{0} \rule{5pt}{0pt} \\ \rule[-10pt]{0pt}{25pt} \rule{5pt}{0pt} \mathbf{0} & -\mathbf{1} \\ \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \rule[-5pt]{0pt}{20pt} \rule{5pt}{0pt} \mathbf{X} \rule{5pt}{0pt} \\ \rule[-10pt]{0pt}{25pt} \rule{5pt}{0pt} \mathbf{Y} \\ \end{pmatrix} \end{eqnarray}\]

From here: At the foundation of this triangle lie the incorrect one-particle spectrum, the lack of integer discontinuity, and the self-interaction error. At the top level, these failures manifest themselves in incorrect charge transfer and Rydberg excitation energies, along with poor activation barriers. The latter includes an incorrect one-particle excitation energy spectrum, the lack of an integer discontinuity when adding and removing an electron, and the pervasive self-interaction error.
